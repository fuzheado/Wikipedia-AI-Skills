# Key Wikipedia Policies for Biography Writing

## BLP — Biographies of Living Persons

**Full policy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Biographies_of_living_persons

### Core Rules
1. **Any unsourced or poorly sourced contentious material about a living person must be removed immediately.** Do not leave it in the article even temporarily, even in a "notes" section.
2. **Be very firm** about high-quality sources. For living persons, every statement that could be contentious needs a reliable source. LBL (libel) is a legal issue.
3. **Do not include** private information (home address, phone number, email, exact date of birth without a source, etc.) for a living person.
4. **Give due weight** — do not overemphasize minor controversies, lawsuits, or personal matters out of proportion to their significance.
5. **Use neutral language** — avoid loaded terms where possible ("controversial," "alleged," "scandal," "rocky," etc.).
6. **Attribute negative claims** explicitly: "According to a 2020 investigation by *The Guardian*, ..." not "Smith was involved in ..."
7. **Harm principle** — If publishing a fact could cause harm to the subject and it is not essential to the biography, omit it.

### Common BLP Violations to Catch
- "Allegedly" — used to insert unsourced negative content
- Negative content sourced only to tabloids or gossip sites
- Birth dates without a reliable source
- Family details (spouse, children) without reliable sources
- Living person's email, phone, address
- Overemphasis on lawsuits, divorces, or personal disputes

## NPOV — Neutral Point of View

**Full policy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view

### Core Rules
1. **Avoid promotional language.** Words to watch: `renowned`, `leading`, `pioneering`, `legendary`, `world-class`, `visionary`, `guru`, `iconic`, `famous`, `best-known`, `highly-respected`.
2. **Present claims as claims, not facts.** "Smith argues that ..." not "Smith proved that ...". "According to X, ..." or "X has described the work as ..."
3. **Attribute opinions.** When writing about what someone thinks or believes, attribute it: "Smith believes that climate change is ..." not "Climate change is ..."
4. **Give proportional weight.** Major views in the field get substantial space; fringe or minority views get minimal or no space.
5. **Do not take sides** in disputes. Describe both sides' positions fairly without implying one is correct.
6. **Avoid weasel words.** "Some people say ...", "It is widely believed ...", "Critics claim ..." — these need specific attribution or removal.

### NPOV Checklist
- [ ] No promotional adjectives
- [ ] Controversial claims attributed
- [ ] All sides of disputes represented
- [ ] Proportional weight given
- [ ] Weasel words removed

## NOR — No Original Research

**Full policy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research

### Core Rules
1. **No synthesis** — Do not connect information across multiple sources to imply a conclusion that neither source explicitly states. Example: Source A says Smith was in City X on Monday. Source B says a crime occurred in City X on Monday. You cannot write "Smith may have been involved in the crime."
2. **No analysis** — Every fact in the article must be traceable directly to a published reliable source. Your own analysis, interpretation, or opinion has no place.
3. **No interpretation** — Let the sources speak. Do not add your own reading of events, subtext, or "between the lines" conclusions.
4. **Primary sources are acceptable** for straightforward factual claims (e.g., the subject's birth date from a birth certificate), but not for interpretive analysis.

### NOR Checklist
- [ ] No connections made between sources that neither source states
- [ ] No original analysis or interpretation
- [ ] Every fact traceable to a source
- [ ] Primary sources used only for straightforward facts

## V — Verifiability

**Full policy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability

### Core Rules
1. **Verifiability does not mean truth.** The threshold for inclusion is verifiability against a published reliable source, not whether the editor believes it is true.
2. **Use only published, reliable sources** with editorial oversight. Blogs, social media, and self-published sources are generally not acceptable.
3. **Self-published sources may be used** for information about the author themselves (e.g., their own website for their biography), but not for claims about third parties.
4. **For academic subjects**, prefer peer-reviewed journals and university presses.
5. **For current events**, prefer major news outlets with established fact-checking processes.
6. **For living persons**, the bar is higher — every potentially contentious statement needs a high-quality source.

### Source Reliability Guide

| Source Type | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Peer-reviewed journal | ✅ Excellent | Gold standard |
| Major newspaper (NYT, Guardian, Reuters, AP) | ✅ Excellent | Fact-checking, editorial oversight |
| University press book | ✅ Excellent | Peer-reviewed |
| Government publication | ✅ Good | Official, authoritative |
| Trade magazine | ⚠️ Acceptable | May have bias |
| Subject's own website | ⚠️ Acceptable | For basic facts only |
| Personal blog | ❌ Not acceptable | No editorial oversight |
| Social media | ❌ Not acceptable | Unless the subject's own and uncontroversial |
| Wikipedia mirror | ❌ Circular | Cannot cite Wikipedia itself |
| Sponsored content | ❌ Not acceptable | Not independent |

## GNG — General Notability Guideline

**Full policy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability

A topic is presumed to be notable if it has received **significant coverage** in **reliable sources** that are **independent of the subject**.

- **Significant coverage** — Addresses the topic directly and in detail. More than a trivial mention.
- **Multiple sources** — More than one source is expected.
- **Reliable** — Sources with editorial integrity.
- **Independent** — Not the subject themselves, their publisher, or their PR team.
- **Presumed** — Notability is presumed, not guaranteed. If sources exist, the article can be written.

## Snapshot: What to Check Before Publishing

1. **BLP check** — Any living person? Remove unsourced contentious material.
2. **NPOV check** — Any promotional language? Attribute opinions? Proportional weight?
3. **NOR check** — Any synthesis? Any original analysis?
4. **Verifiability check** — Every fact sourced? Sources reliable?
5. **Notability check** — Subject meets GNG or an SNG?
